\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We re-examined the comparative performance of linear and tree-based models for one-step-ahead realized-volatility forecasting on the HAR feature set. Using default hyperparameters drawn from prior literature, the tree ensembles (XGBoost, LightGBM, random forest) appear markedly inferior to ridge regression, echoing a pattern widely reported in the volatility-forecasting literature. We showed that this ranking is an artifact of default-hyperparameter choice. A Bayesian search over standard hyperparameter spaces using Optuna's TPE sampler, parallelized across two HPC clusters, produces tree configurations that are competitive with or better than ridge: tuned LightGBM attains the lowest QLIKE in the comparison, tuned XGBoost closes most of the gap, and tuned random forest ties ridge to within 0.004 QLIKE units. The practical implication is that claims about linear-versus-tree performance on HAR features are fragile in the absence of a tuning protocol, and that the feature-interaction capacity of tree ensembles contributes meaningful forecasting signal that depth-3, 100-estimator defaults cannot express.

Open questions for future work include (i) whether tuned trees continue to improve on ridge when informative exogenous subgroups are added to the feature set, (ii) whether the apparent LightGBM advantage over XGBoost survives larger tuning budgets or is an artifact of the 30-trial LightGBM study at the time of writing, and (iii) how tuned tree ensembles compare to neural architectures such as the attention-based sequence models that motivated this comparison.
